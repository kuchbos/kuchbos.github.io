Oleg Sobchuk: Macroevolution of the Arts
About Publications Research

Does literature advance “one funeral at a time”?

Literature, as any artistic domain, is the field of constant innovation. New book genres, and sub-genres, pop up every several years. Why does this happen? Do these new genres reflect some important changes in the broader cultural and political landscape? Or do they reflect something else: a constant pendulum of demography? Could it be that the chnage in literature reflects the turnover of generations? Together with Bret Beheim, we tackle this question using a dataset with dozens of thousands of books.

Do music innovators become music stars?

What brings artistic success? Talent? Hard work? Luck? Or, maybe… innovativeness? Turns out innovativeness does play a big role – at least in music. In this paper, my colleagues and I test whether we can see “first-mover advantage” in music genres. That is, do musicians who are early members of new genres, end up being more popular?

Phylogenies of literature

What is the best way of representing the transmission of literary traditions? In this project I lead an interdisciplinary to team to develop the new methods for building historical networks of literary evolution, which one might call “phylogenies of literature”

What are the best ways of computing genres?

We live during the boom of text mining. Every day some new algorithm or tool is introduced. So, it becomes ever more important to compare these tools and make competitions between them. In a new paper, Artjoms Šela and I create a “race of algorithms”, each of which must classify a bunch of texts in 4 known genres (detective, fantasy, sci-fi, and romance) as close as possible to the original classification. What is the best algo? We answer this and also discover a bunch of unexpected things: some of the “true and tried” methods are not as good as one might think.

Are modern movies more complex than old ones?

Are modern movies better than old ones? Is Lawrence of Arabia superior to Dune? One might say: fighting over tastes is tasteless. True. But this question isn’t purely about taste. If old movies are so good, why do we keep watching new ones? This question has always bothered me, and so Peeter Tinits and I decided to operationalize it. Could it be that modern movies are more technologically superior, similarly to how modern airplanes are more superior to old ones? We decided to test this using a large dataset of film crews and jobs.
